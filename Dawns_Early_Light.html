The Ramparts Have Fallen

…but history held firm.

In reality, Fort McHenry endured. Despite 1,500–1,800 British rounds over 25 hours, its walls, water batteries, and a line of deliberately sunken ships kept the fleet at bay — only 4 defenders were killed and 24 wounded.

O Say, Can You See…

7:00 A.M., September 14, 1814 — the guns fall silent

As the smoke clears, Armistead's men haul down the tattered storm flag and raise Mary Pickersgill's great garrison flag — 30 by 42 feet, 15 stars and 15 stripes. Eight miles down the Patapsco, watching from a truce ship, the lawyer Francis Scott Key sees it and begins scribbling verses on the back of a letter.

And the rockets' red glare, the bombs bursting in air,
Gave proof through the night that our flag was still there;
O say does that star-spangled banner yet wave
O'er the land of the free and the home of the brave?

What really happened:

  • Key's poem, "Defence of Fort M'Henry," was printed on Sept. 17, 1814, and set to the English tune "To Anacreon in Heaven."
  • It became the official U.S. national anthem only in 1931, 117 years later.
  • The garrison flag — with one star snipped away by souvenir hunters — hangs today in the Smithsonian's National Museum of American History.
